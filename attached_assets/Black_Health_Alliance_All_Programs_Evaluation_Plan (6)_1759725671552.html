Black Health Alliance — All Programs draft evaluation plan

Created on 2025-10-06 by LogicalOutcomes Evaluation Planner

This evaluation plan is designed to be a living document, supporting Black Health Alliance in its commitment to continuous improvement and demonstrating the impact of All Programs. The approach is collaborative and aims to provide actionable insights for program staff, management, and funders. It is guided by the principles of practical, utilization-focused evaluation.

This plan does not include citations or research references. It is based on the LogicalOutcomes Evaluation Planning Handbook by Gillian Kerr and Sophie Llewelyn, which describes a structured process that relies on general evidence about effective nonprofit programs, supported by an in-depth web search.

Program summary and analysis

Summary of the program

According to Black Health Alliance’s website, the organization is a community-led registered charity working to improve the health and well-being of Black communities in Canada. BHA centres Black communities in defining challenges and co-designing solutions, and focuses its work through three pillars: Health Promotion & Community Engagement; Research and Policy; and Strategy, Design & Implementation. Strategic focus areas include the social determinants of health, health inequities, anti-Black racism, and building connected communities.

Based on the program model research from the previous analysis, “All Programs” functions as a set of coordinated initiatives that convene partners, co-design solutions with Black community members, develop and mobilize evidence, and support policy and systems change. The program aims to reduce health inequities, improve access and quality of services, and strengthen connected communities across Canada.

Program overview

According to the program description, BHA addresses key issues impacting Black communities in Canada by combining community engagement and health promotion with community-based research, policy work, and support for strategy, design, and implementation. BHA positions Black community members as co-leaders in defining problems and crafting solutions, and it works with multisector partners to implement those solutions.

Programs of this type typically provide a mix of direct engagement (e.g., forums, campaigns, navigation), knowledge products (e.g., research reports, policy briefs), and implementation supports (e.g., co-design sprints, technical assistance, training). The general journey for a Black community member starts with accessible engagement or outreach, opportunities to inform program design, and participation in health promotion activities. For partner organizations and systems, the journey includes engagement, co-design of changes, training and coaching to adopt equity practices, and support to collect and use race-based and equity data ethically.

Based on the previous analysis, intended outcomes flow from increased awareness and trust, to adoption of equity-informed policies and practices, to sustained improvements in access, quality, and health outcomes for Black communities. BHA’s strategic plan (2022–2025) guides multi-year initiatives across these pillars.

Activities

Activities are organized using Black Health Alliance’s own pillars and language.

• Health promotion & community engagement

  • Hosting community forums, dialogues, and listening sessions
  • Co-creating and delivering culturally relevant health promotion campaigns
  • Mobilizing community ambassadors and peer outreach
  • Providing navigation, referral, and accompaniment to services
  • Convening co-design workshops with Black community members

• Research and policy

  • Conducting community-based and participatory research projects
  • Developing evidence syntheses, reports, and policy briefs
  • Supporting ethical race-based and equity data collection and governance
  • Engaging in policy analysis and advocacy with decision-makers

• Strategy, design & implementation

  • Running co-design sprints and prototyping solutions with partners
  • Providing implementation support, training, and coaching to organizations
  • Brokering cross-sector partnerships and shared measurement
  • Supporting quality improvement and change management in partner sites

• Cross-cutting capacity building and knowledge mobilization

  • Delivering anti-Black racism and cultural safety training
  • Developing and sharing toolkits, curricula, and resources
  • Maintaining data stewardship, privacy, and evaluation systems
  • Disseminating knowledge through events, dashboards, and communications

Key management processes

Several management processes must be properly implemented for an effective program. The following processes are defined in the LogicalOutcomes Evaluation Planning Handbook, supplemented by a web search and adapted to Black Health Alliance.

• Feedback and quality control: Establish a continuous feedback loop that includes Black community member input from forums, surveys, and listening sessions; partner feedback from debriefs; and routine quality checks on training, co-design sessions, and knowledge products. Use rapid-cycle learning (e.g., short pulse surveys, post-event reflections) to inform program adjustments.

• Participatory decision-making: Engage Black community members as co-designers and advisors with paid roles and clear decision-making power. Use structured advisory groups and participatory sense-making sessions to shape priorities, interpret findings, and guide adaptations across all pillars.

• Staff development: Provide ongoing training in anti-Black racism, cultural safety, trauma- and violence-informed approaches, CBPR methods, facilitation, implementation coaching, and data ethics. Pair training with mentorship, peer learning, and reflective practice.

• Resource management: Track budgets, staff effort, and in-kind contributions across initiatives to ensure sustainability and alignment with the 2022–2025 strategic plan. Prioritize resources for community compensation, accessibility supports, and data stewardship.

• Partnership management: Maintain MOUs, shared goals, and clear roles with health, social, education, and government partners. Facilitate regular partnership meetings, shared measurement, and joint problem-solving to align activities and avoid duplication.

• Ethical race-based data governance: Implement transparent consent, privacy, and data minimization protocols; establish community oversight for data use; ensure compliance with relevant laws and standards; and provide staff training on data ethics and equity.

• Knowledge mobilization and communications: Plan targeted dissemination for each product (briefs, toolkits, dashboards), tailoring messages to Black community members, providers, and policy audiences. Track reach, engagement, and use to improve future outputs.

Areas of evaluation focus

Based on the priorities and risks above, the evaluation will focus on the following areas:

  1. Black community member engagement and retention: Measure levels of active participation and follow-through across forums, training, and co-design to understand engagement drivers and barriers.
  2. Program accessibility: Assess language, mobility, digital, geographic, and scheduling barriers; track use of accessibility supports such as stipends, childcare, and interpretation.
  3. Cultural appropriateness: Evaluate how well activities and materials align with the diverse cultures within Black communities and their lived experiences, including anti-Black racism and intersectionality.
  4. Outcome achievement: Track progress on short-, mid-, and long-term outcomes across the three pillars, including access, policy/practice changes, and health equity indicators.
  5. Partnership effectiveness: Assess partnership quality, shared measures, alignment of activities, and the degree to which partners adopt and sustain equity practices.
  6. Staff satisfaction and turnover: Monitor workload, burnout risk, and professional development to sustain high-quality delivery.
  7. Safety and well-being: Monitor psychological safety and culturally safe practice during engagements, and ensure ethical data practices protect privacy and reduce harm.

Program evaluation plan

This section outlines a full evaluation plan to evaluate All Programs, including roles, responsibilities and meeting agendas.

Evaluation objectives

  1. Meet Black community member needs and help Black community members achieve their goals.
  2. Achieve program goals and outcomes.
  3. Improve program quality and fidelity.
  4. Increase responsiveness to Black community members in the way activities and supports are provided.
  5. Increase accessibility and equity in service provision (as defined by the program; e.g., for persons with disabilities, people living on low incomes, and diverse Black diasporas and underserved groups).
  6. Increase responsiveness to key interest groups (e.g., the broader Black community, employees, partners, funders, donors).

Evaluation questions

  1. What activities are provided by All Programs?
  2. To what extent is All Programs being implemented as designed and meeting quality standards?
  3. What are the characteristics of the populations served by the program?
  4. What do Black community members think about BHA activities and supports and what are their suggestions?
  5. What do staff and other key interest groups think about BHA activities and supports and what are their suggestions?
  6. To what extent are Black community members meeting their goals?
  7. What short-term and mid-term changes are Black community members experiencing?
  8. What evidence suggests the program is contributing to its desired long-term impact?

Evaluation phases

Overview

This evaluation will follow a compressed timeline to provide rapid, useful feedback. The phases are designed to build on each other, from foundational planning and engagement to data analysis and reporting. The intent is to focus evaluation resources as much as possible on understanding and responding to interest group perspectives and on improving program quality.

The evaluation project includes the following activities divided into four overlapping phases:

  • Planning and initiation: Activities 1 – 5 (duration: 3 to 8 weeks)
  • Design and development: Activities 5 – 9 (duration: 1 to 12 weeks)
  • Implementation and analysis: Activities 9 – 11 (ongoing until project completion)
  • Communication and discussion: Activities 11 – 12 (duration: 3 to 6 weeks)

Phase 1. Engage the project sponsor, team members, and key decision-makers

  • Confirm evaluation scope, budget and delivery date for the final report.
  • Define the main roles and responsibilities for the evaluation project, including the manager who is overseeing the project and who will be taking the recommendations to senior management.
  • Identify and invite members to an Evaluation Advisory Committee.
  • Define the timelines, resources (including staff effort for interviews and data collection) and the decision process for approval of final report.

Phase 2. Define evaluation objectives and questions

  • Facilitate a meeting with the advisory committee to discuss and revise the evaluation objectives and questions.
  • Ensure evaluation questions are relevant to staff, meaningful to Black community members, and credible to funders.
  • Confirm or revise the specific activities that are delivered by the program and that are essential to the program. Include direct services to Black community members as well as other essential elements such as collaboration with other organizations, staff training and financial management.
  • Confirm or revise the key interest groups that should be engaged in data collection and/or discussion of the results.
  • Finalize the evaluation framework, confirming that the indicators are measurable and meaningful.

Phase 3. Develop and test data collection tools

  • Review and possibly adapt current client record system if one exists to assess whether it can generate reports for Black community member goals, milestones and activities. For example, every Black community member should be asked what they hope to get from the services, and if feasible asked later if they achieved their goals.
  • Draft all data collection tools (surveys, interview guides, tracking sheets). If possible, adapt and customize them from existing validated data collection tools to save time and improve validity.
  • Pilot test the tools with a small group of Black community members and staff to ensure clarity and cultural appropriateness.
  • Refine tools based on pilot feedback.

Phase 4. Collect and manage data

  • Train staff or designated data collectors on protocols to ensure data quality and consistency.
  • Deploy surveys, conduct interviews, and analyze client records as per the evaluation framework.
  • Ensure ethical protocols, including informed consent and data confidentiality, are strictly followed.
  • Frequent interim reports will assess whether the data appears to be accurate and whether it captures feedback from defined interest groups and demographic groups.
  • If necessary, additional demographic segments may be targeted with additional interviews or financial incentives.

Phase 5. Analyze data and interpret findings

  • Clean and analyze quantitative and qualitative data.
  • Deliver reports frequently to appropriate staff and interest groups with a focus on recommended actions. Emerging findings may be tested with follow-up interviews or more detailed reports.
  • Notes from staff meetings will be reviewed for evidence of actions that have been taken or recommendations that have been made as a result of the reports.
  • Hold a "sense-making" session with the advisory committee and staff to interpret the initial findings, discuss implications, and co-develop recommendations. This participatory step is crucial for ensuring the results are understood and owned by the team.

Phase 6. Communicate findings and facilitate use

  • Draft a comprehensive evaluation report with a clear executive summary.
  • Develop tailored communication materials for different groups (e.g., one-page summary for the board, presentation for staff, stories for a newsletter).
  • Facilitate a final meeting to discuss the report and create an action plan for implementing the recommendations.

Roles and responsibilities

It is necessary to involve senior managers and decision-makers in the evaluation process so that the resulting information will be relevant and credible to them. They should be engaged at three stages:

  • Defining objectives, roles and key interest groups at the beginning of the evaluation.
  • Engaging with data as soon as it starts to be collected and then occasionally throughout the project.
  • Engaging with the final recommendations and action plans near the end of the evaluation.

In addition, representatives of the organization need to be involved more deeply in:

  • Defining the program services and key processes.
  • Defining project timelines and tracking quality.
  • Configuring the client record system and refining the data collection tools.

And of course members of key interest groups will be asked for input as part of the data collection activities.

There are four essential roles for a successful evaluation project:

Sponsor (generally a senior manager who will be responsible for implementing recommendations)

  • Provide guidance regarding overall objectives and constraints of project
  • Liaise with the organization's senior management and manage organizational expectations and scope issues as appropriate
  • Communicate with internal and external interest groups regarding project progress
  • Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Liaison
  • Approve significant changes to the project scope, timeline, budget, or quality if required
  • Review and approve project documents and other deliverables

Liaison (generally a manager at the organization reporting to the Project Sponsor)

  • Act as the primary contact person with the evaluator
  • Liaise with the Project Sponsor and take on their responsibilities as delegated
  • Act as a project manager from the organization's side, e.g., scheduling meetings with program staff, negotiating with I.T. staff

Project Owner (generally a senior external evaluator)

  • Provide project leadership for the evaluation as a whole
  • Define project methodologies and advise on strategic issues
  • Author, review and approve project documents as assigned
  • Manage and resolve team-level risks, issues, and changes
  • Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Project Sponsor
  • Review and provide detailed feedback regarding all project documents and deliverables

Project Manager (generally an external evaluator reporting to the Project Owner)

  • Act as liaison to the organization for operational issues
  • Monitor project scope, quality, schedule, resources, costs and risks
  • Coordinate implementation of project work
  • Ensure project plan, schedule, and budget are up-to-date; detect and manage discrepancies
  • Report risks, delays and problems to Project Owner and Project Sponsor as they arise
  • Author, review and approve project documents as assigned to ensure the quality standards are met
  • Arrange and follow-up on team meetings
  • Manage and contribute to data collection, analysis and report writing
  • Provide leadership and manage work as appropriate

Program staff (one or more representatives of the people who will actually implement service improvements and experience changes in their workplace)

Other common roles include I.T. specialists, program Black community members and representatives from key constituencies.
