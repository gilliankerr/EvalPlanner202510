Black Health Alliance — All Programs Draft Evaluation Plan

Created on 2025-10-07 by LogicalOutcomes Evaluation Planner

This draft evaluation plan aims to support Black Health Alliance in a process of continuous improvement that will also demonstrate the impact of All Programs. The evaluation approach is designed to provide useful information for program staff, management, and funders with minimal time spent in initial planning, logic modelling, creating an evaluation framework, and so on. It helps evaluation teams move quickly to data collection, analysis and engagement by streamlining the planning phase.

This plan does not include citations or research references. It is based on the LogicalOutcomes Evaluation Planning Handbook by Gillian Kerr and Sophie Llewelyn, which describes a structured process that relies on general evidence about effective nonprofit programs, supported by an in-depth web search about the program itself.

We recommend that users also carry out a quick literature review to check that the program’s design is consistent with research and evidence about its effectiveness with the target population. For a free research scan we suggest Consensus. Ask something like, ‘What are likely to be essential program delivery elements and critical success factors for (type of program) serving (target population)? Program summary and analysis

Summary of the program

Black Health Alliance (BHA) is a community-led registered charity working to improve the health and well-being of Black communities in Canada. BHA centres Black communities in defining challenges and co-designing solutions across health promotion and community engagement, research and policy, and strategy, design and implementation.

All Programs aims to reduce health inequities by addressing social determinants of health, anti-Black racism, and fragmented services. BHA serves Black community members (youth, adults, elders, newcomers, and families) and works with partners across health, social, education, and policy systems. Key activities include community outreach and health promotion, peer and lived-experience engagement, participatory research and knowledge mobilization, policy advocacy, provider training, implementation support, and coalition-building to foster connected communities. See: https://blackhealthalliance.ca/

Program overview

Scope and components

  • Approach pillars: Health Promotion & Community Engagement; Research and Policy; Strategy, Design & Implementation.
  • Strategic focus areas: Social Determinants of Health, Health Inequities, Anti-Black Racism, and Building Connected Communities.
  • Geographic remit: Canada-wide with many partnerships in Ontario, with an intent to scale effective models.

How All Programs works

  • Community-led engagement: BHA organizes outreach, workshops, and campaigns to build health literacy, trust, and navigation skills among Black community members. Co-design sessions and lived-experience leadership surface barriers and shape solutions that fit local contexts.
  • Evidence and policy: BHA conducts community-based research, translates knowledge into practical tools (briefs, reports, toolkits), and advances policy and funding aligned with anti-racism and equity.
  • Strategy, design, and implementation: BHA provides technical assistance to providers and systems to redesign pathways (e.g., referrals and navigation), trains staff in culturally safe and anti-racist practice, and supports implementation and evaluation to scale promising practices.
  • Building connected communities: BHA convenes coalitions and networks, aligns partners around shared goals and indicators, and strengthens infrastructure for collective impact.

Participant journeys

  • For Black community members: Outreach and invitation → culturally tailored workshops or campaigns → optional peer navigation and referrals → follow-up to support service access and continuity → feedback on experience and outcomes.
  • For providers and partners: Readiness and needs assessment → training and coaching → pathway redesign and pilot implementation → fidelity checks and adaptation → shared measurement and learning → policy/practice adoption and scale-up.

Activities

  • Health promotion & community engagement
    • Attending culturally tailored workshops and outreach events
    • Receiving navigation support from peer or lived-experience leaders
    • Participating in campaigns that promote screening, mental health, and preventive care
    • Joining listening sessions and feedback circles to shape services
  • Co-design and community leadership
    • Participating in co-design sessions to define problems and solutions
    • Contributing lived-experience to tools, materials, and prototypes
    • Serving on advisory groups with honoraria and clear roles
  • Research & policy
    • Joining community-based studies and surveys
    • Reviewing plain-language briefs on findings and implications
    • Providing input to policy consultations and public forums
    • Using toolkits and resources developed from research
  • Strategy, design & implementation support
    • Receiving provider training in anti-racist, culturally safe practice
    • Participating in pathway redesign (e.g., more accessible referrals)
    • Receiving implementation coaching and quality improvement support
    • Using practice guidelines, SOPs, and simple fidelity tools
  • Building connected communities
    • Joining coalition meetings and community convenings
    • Aligning on shared goals, indicators, and data dashboards
    • Sharing stories of impact and lessons learned across partners

Key management processes

Several management processes must be properly implemented to make for an effective program.

  • Feedback and quality control: Establish a simple, ongoing feedback loop across all pillars. Use short pulse checks after workshops, quick debriefs after navigation encounters, and partner feedback forms after trainings. Track suggestions and actions taken in a shared log to inform continuous improvement.
  • Participatory decision-making: Engage Black community members and lived-experience leaders in co-design, advisory groups, and sense-making sessions. Ensure paid participation, accessible formats, and transparent follow-up on how feedback changes activities.
  • Staff development: Provide continuous training for staff and peer navigators in cultural safety, trauma-informed practice, anti-racism/anti-oppression, implementation support, and ethical community-based research. Offer reflective practice and supervision to prevent burnout.
  • Resource management: Monitor budgets, staff time, and partner contributions. Use simple dashboards to track delivery against the 2022–2025 strategic plan, including milestones for pilots, research outputs, and policy efforts.
  • Partnership management: Use MOUs, readiness assessments, and regular check-ins with health, social, education, and community partners. Clarify roles for pathway changes, data sharing, and shared measurement.
  • Community data governance and privacy: Apply purpose-limited consent, role-based access, and de-identification. Use community oversight for race-based data, and maintain clear data-sharing agreements.
  • Safety and accessibility protocols: Conduct risk assessments for community events, ensure accessibility (transport, childcare, translation, virtual options), and maintain incident reporting and follow-up procedures.

Areas of evaluation focus

  1. Black community member engagement and retention: Track participation, repeat attendance, and follow-through on navigation and referrals.
  2. Staff satisfaction and turnover: Monitor workload, supervision quality, and retention of peer navigators and staff.
  3. Program accessibility: Assess barriers (transport, childcare, language, timing, digital access) and the effectiveness of supports offered.
  4. Safety and well-being: Track and review any safety incidents; monitor the well-being of community members and peer staff involved.
  5. Cultural appropriateness: Assess culturally safe experience, trust, and relevance of activities co-designed with Black communities.
  6. Outcome achievement: Monitor short-, mid-, and long-term outcomes (e.g., health literacy, navigation self-efficacy, referral completion, adoption of anti-racist practices).
  7. Partnership effectiveness: Evaluate readiness, fidelity to pathway redesign, and the quality of collaboration and shared measurement.

Program evaluation plan

This section outlines a full evaluation plan to evaluate All Programs, including roles, responsibilities and meeting agendas.

Evaluation objectives

  1. Meet Black community member needs and help Black community members achieve their goals.
  2. Achieve program goals and outcomes.
  3. Improve program quality and fidelity.
  4. Increase responsiveness to Black community members in the way activities are provided.
  5. Increase accessibility and equity in service provision (as defined by the program itself; e.g., for persons with disabilities, people living on low incomes, from defined racial or ethnic groups, for underserved groups in the target population).
  6. Increase responsiveness to key interest groups (as defined by the program; e.g., to the broader community served, employees, volunteers, funders, donors).

Evaluation questions

  1. What activities are provided by All Programs?
  2. To what extent is All Programs being implemented as designed and meeting quality standards?
  3. What are the characteristics of the populations served by the program?
  4. What do Black community members think about activities and supports and what are their suggestions?
  5. What do staff and other key interest groups think about activities and supports and what are their suggestions?
  6. To what extent are Black community members meeting their goals?
  7. What short-term and mid-term changes are Black community members experiencing?
  8. What evidence suggests the program is contributing to its desired long-term impact?

Evaluation phases

This evaluation will be conducted in four overlapping phases to ensure a structured and participatory process.

Overview

This evaluation will follow a compressed timeline to provide rapid, useful feedback. The phases are designed to build on each other, from foundational planning and engagement to data analysis and reporting. The intent is to focus evaluation resources as much as possible on understanding and responding to interest group perspectives and on improving program quality.

The evaluation project includes the following activities divided into four overlapping phases:

  • Planning and initiation: Activities 1 – 5 (duration: 3 to 8 weeks)
  • Design and development: Activities 5 – 9 (duration: 1 to 12 weeks)
  • Implementation and analysis: Activities 9 – 11 (ongoing until project completion)
  • Communication and discussion: Activities 11 – 12 (duration: 3 to 6 weeks)

Phase 1. Engage the project sponsor, team members, and key decision-makers

  • Confirm evaluation scope, budget and delivery date for the final report.
  • Define the main roles and responsibilities for the evaluation project, including the manager who is overseeing the project and who will be taking the recommendations to senior management.
  • Identify and invite members to an Evaluation Advisory Committee.
  • Define the timelines, resources (including staff effort for interviews and data collection) and the decision process for approval of final report.

Phase 2. Define evaluation objectives and questions

  • Facilitate a meeting with the advisory committee to discuss and revise the evaluation objectives and questions.
  • Ensure evaluation questions are relevant to staff, meaningful to Black community members, and credible to funders.
  • Confirm or revise the specific activities that are delivered by the program and that are essential to the program. Include direct services to Black community members as well as other essential elements such as collaboration with other organizations, staff training and financial management.
  • Confirm or revise the key interest groups that should be engaged in data collection and/or discussion of the results.
  • Finalize the evaluation framework, confirming that the indicators are measurable and meaningful.

Phase 3. Develop and test data collection tools

  • Review and possibly adapt current client record system if one exists to assess whether it can generate reports for Black community member goals, milestones and activities. For example, every Black community member should be asked what they hope to get from the activities, and if feasible asked later if they achieved their goals.
  • Draft all data collection tools (surveys, interview guides, tracking sheets). If possible, adapt and customize them from existing validated data collection tools to save time and improve validity.
  • Pilot test the tools with a small group of Black community members and staff to ensure clarity and cultural appropriateness.
  • Refine tools based on pilot feedback.

Phase 4. Collect and manage data

  • Train staff or designated data collectors on protocols to ensure data quality and consistency.
  • Deploy surveys, conduct interviews, and analyze client records as per the evaluation framework.
  • Ensure ethical protocols, including informed consent and data confidentiality, are strictly followed.
  • Frequent interim reports will assess whether the data appears to be accurate and whether it captures feedback from defined interest groups and demographic groups.
  • If necessary, additional demographic segments may be targeted with additional interviews or financial incentives.

Phase 5. Analyze data and interpret findings

  • Clean and analyze quantitative and qualitative data.
  • Deliver reports frequently to appropriate staff and interest groups with a focus on recommended actions. Emerging findings may be tested with follow-up interviews or more detailed reports.
  • Notes from staff meetings will be reviewed for evidence of actions that have been taken or recommendations that have been made as a result of the reports.
  • Hold a "sense-making" session with the advisory committee and staff to interpret the initial findings, discuss implications, and co-develop recommendations. This participatory step is crucial for ensuring the results are understood and owned by the team.

Phase 6. Communicate findings and facilitate use

  • Draft a comprehensive evaluation report with a clear executive summary.
  • Develop tailored communication materials for different groups (e.g., one-page summary for the board, presentation for staff, stories for a newsletter).
  • Facilitate a final meeting to discuss the report and create an action plan for implementing the recommendations.

Roles and responsibilities

It is necessary to involve senior managers and decision-makers in the evaluation process so that the resulting information will be relevant and credible to them. They should be engaged at three stages:

  • Defining objectives, roles and key interest groups at the beginning of the evaluation.
  • Engaging with data as soon as it starts to be collected and then occasionally throughout the project.
  • Engaging with the final recommendations and action plans near the end of the evaluation.

In addition, representatives of the organization need to be involved more deeply in:

  • Defining the program services and key processes.
  • Defining project timelines and tracking quality.
  • Configuring the client record system and refining the data collection tools.

And of course members of key interest groups will be asked for input as part of the data collection activities.

There are four essential roles for a successful evaluation project:

Sponsor (generally a senior manager who will be responsible for implementing recommendations)

  • Provide guidance regarding overall objectives and constraints of project
  • Liaise with the organization's senior management and manage organizational expectations and scope issues as appropriate
  • Communicate with internal and external interest groups regarding project progress
  • Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Liaison
  • Approve significant changes to the project scope, timeline, budget, or quality if required
  • Review and approve project documents and other deliverables

Liaison (generally a manager at the organization reporting to the Project Sponsor)

  • Act as the primary contact person with the evaluator
  • Liaise with the Project Sponsor and take on their responsibilities as delegated
  • Act as a project manager from the organization's side, e.g., scheduling meetings with program staff, negotiating with I.T. staff

Project Owner (generally a senior external evaluator)

  • Provide project leadership for the evaluation as a whole
  • Define project methodologies and advise on strategic issues
  • Author, review and approve project documents as assigned
  • Manage and resolve team-level risks, issues, and changes
  • Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Project Sponsor
  • Review and provide detailed feedback regarding all project documents and deliverables

Project Manager (generally an external evaluator reporting to the Project Owner)

  • Act as liaison to the organization for operational issues
  • Monitor project scope, quality, schedule, resources, costs and risks
  • Coordinate implementation of project work
  • Ensure project plan, schedule, and budget are up-to-date; detect and manage discrepancies
  • Report risks, delays and problems to Project Owner and Project Sponsor as they arise
  • Author, review and approve project documents as assigned to ensure the quality standards are met
  • Arrange and follow-up on team meetings
  • Manage and contribute to data collection, analysis and report writing
  • Provide leadership and manage work as appropriate

Program staff (one or more representatives of the people who will actually implement service improvements and experience changes in their workplace)

Other common roles include I.T. specialists, program Black community members and representatives from key constituencies.
