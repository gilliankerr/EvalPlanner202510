Program summary and analysis

Summary of the program

According to the program description, Black Health Alliance (BHA) is a community-led registered charity that works in partnership to improve the health and well-being of Black communities in Canada. BHA focuses on four strategic areas: Social Determinants of Health, Health Inequities, Anti-Black Racism, and Building Connected Communities. Its work is delivered through three pillars: Health Promotion & Community Engagement; Research and Policy; and Strategy, Design & Implementation.

Based on the program model research from the previous analysis, BHA‚Äôs ‚ÄúAll Programs‚Äù portfolio serves Black community members across the life course, including immigrants, refugees, and Canadian-born Black populations. BHA centers Black communities to define problems and co-design solutions, mobilizes partners and investments, and translates community-informed research into policy and practice change to improve equitable access, care experiences, and health outcomes.

Program overview

According to the program description, BHA engages Black communities to surface challenges and barriers affecting health and well-being and provides a platform for a common voice to address systemic problems. BHA convenes partners, co-designs solutions, and supports implementation to achieve measurable, sustained change. The organization is guided by a 2022‚Äì2025 strategic plan (‚ÄúTHRIVE‚Äù) that outlines its theory of change, priorities, and actions.

Based on the program model research from the previous analysis, All Programs integrates three core components:

  • Health Promotion & Community Engagement: culturally relevant outreach, education, and navigation supports; community forums and listening sessions; peer and lay health advisor approaches to connect Black community members to resources.
  • Research and Policy: community-based participatory research, data generation and dissemination, race-based data governance, and policy analysis/advocacy to address structural drivers of inequity.
  • Strategy, Design & Implementation: co-design of services with Black community members and partners; provider training on anti-Black racism and cultural safety; implementation support, quality improvement, and shared measurement to scale effective practices.

A typical journey for a Black community member includes connecting with BHA through outreach or a community event, learning about relevant health topics and rights, participating in co-design or feedback activities, and, where available through partners, receiving navigation or referral support to culturally safe services. In parallel, providers and system partners engage with BHA for capacity building, co-design, and implementation support that lead to policy and practice changes benefiting Black community members.

Activities

Based on the program model research from the previous analysis, the following activities reflect how Black community members and partners experience All Programs. Activities are grouped under BHA‚Äôs three pillars.

  • Health Promotion & Community Engagement
    • Attending culturally relevant health education sessions, town halls, and forums
    • Receiving navigation and referral support to services and resources
    • Participating in listening sessions, advisory circles, and co-design workshops
    • Accessing digital resources and communications tailored to Black communities
  • Research and Policy
    • Participating in community-led research (surveys, interviews, focus groups) with informed consent
    • Reviewing accessible summaries of findings and contributing to recommendations
    • Joining policy dialogues, consultations, and advocacy efforts that reflect community priorities
  • Strategy, Design & Implementation
    • Co-designing program models and service improvements with BHA and partners
    • Providing feedback on provider training content (anti-Black racism, cultural safety)
    • Taking part in pilot programs and quality improvement cycles and sharing experiences to refine solutions

Programs of this type typically also include behind-the-scenes activities such as partnership development, shared measurement/data dashboards, and communications to elevate community narratives and drive systems change.

Key management processes

Several management processes must be properly implemented for an effective program. The following processes are defined in the LogicalOutcomes Evaluation Planning Handbook, supplemented by the web search and prior analysis.

  • Feedback and quality control: Establish ongoing feedback loops with Black community members, including listening sessions, post-event quick polls, and optional follow-up calls. Use team debriefs to review feedback, track changes made, and document learnings. Where feasible, embed short reflective questions into activities to capture immediate suggestions.
  • Participatory decision-making: Engage Black community members as paid advisors and co-designers. Use community advisory circles to set priorities for health promotion topics, research questions, and policy positions. Share back results (‚Äúyou said, we did‚Äù) to maintain transparency and trust.
  • Staff development: Provide continuous training and reflective supervision in anti-Black racism, cultural safety, trauma- and resilience-informed practice, community-based research ethics, and facilitation. Offer wellness supports to reduce burnout and vicarious trauma.
  • Resource management: Monitor budgets, staff time, and partner in-kind contributions across multi-year initiatives. Align funding with strategic priorities (2022‚Äì2025 plan), and phase projects to match capacity. Track cost-effectiveness where possible.
  • Partnership management: Maintain MOUs and clear roles with community-based organizations, health and social service providers, and academic partners. Use shared goals, milestones, and simple data-sharing agreements with privacy safeguards. Identify champions within partner organizations.
  • Race-based data governance and privacy: Implement community-governed data protocols with informed consent, data minimization, de-identification, and secure storage. Ensure community approval before public dissemination of disaggregated findings.
  • Cultural responsiveness protocols: Ensure materials and events are culturally relevant, accessible, and safe (e.g., plain language, multilingual options, child care or transportation supports, hybrid delivery). Apply ethical storytelling practices to avoid harm and stigma.

Areas of evaluation focus

Based on the priorities and risks above and the previous analysis, the evaluation will focus on:

  1. Black community member engagement and retention: Measure active participation and follow-through in events, co-design, and navigation supports.
  2. Program accessibility: Assess barriers (e.g., transportation, language, digital access) and supports (child care, stipends) to ensure broad reach across Black communities.
  3. Cultural appropriateness: Evaluate how well activities and materials reflect Black community experiences and expectations for cultural safety.
  4. Outcome achievement: Track progress against short-, mid-, and long-term outcomes across the three pillars.
  5. Partnership effectiveness: Assess quality, clarity, and impact of collaborations with community groups, providers, and academic partners.
  6. Staff satisfaction and turnover: Monitor wellness, workload, and development to prevent burnout.
  7. Safety and well-being: Monitor any safety incidents and apply trauma-informed safeguards during engagement and data collection.

Evaluation objectives

  1. Meet Black community member needs and help Black community members achieve their goals.
  2. Achieve program goals and outcomes.
  3. Improve program quality and fidelity.
  4. Increase responsiveness to Black community members in the way activities and supports are provided.

  6. Increase responsiveness to key interest groups (as defined by the program; e.g., to the broader community served, employees, volunteers, funders, donors).

Evaluation questions

  1. What activities are provided by All Programs?
  2. To what extent is All Programs being implemented as designed and meeting quality standards?
  3. What are the characteristics of the populations served by the program?
  4. What do Black community members think about activities and supports and what are their suggestions?
  5. What do staff and other key interest groups think about activities and supports and what are their suggestions?
  6. To what extent are Black community members meeting their goals?
  7. What short-term and mid-term changes are Black community members experiencing?
  8. What evidence suggests the program is contributing to its desired long-term impact?

Evaluation phases, roles, and agendas

This evaluation will be conducted in four overlapping phases to ensure a structured and participatory process.

Overview

This evaluation will follow a compressed timeline to provide rapid, useful feedback. The phases are designed to build on each other, from foundational planning and engagement to data analysis and reporting. The intent is to focus evaluation resources as much as possible on understanding and responding to interest group perspectives and on improving program quality.

The evaluation project includes the following activities divided into four overlapping phases:

  • Planning and initiation: Activities 1 ‚Äì 5 (duration: 3 to 8 weeks)
  • Design and development: Activities 5 ‚Äì 9 (duration: 1 to 12 weeks)
  • Implementation and analysis: Activities 9 ‚Äì 11 (ongoing until project completion)
  • Communication and discussion: Activities 11 ‚Äì 12 (duration: 3 to 6 weeks)

1. Engage the project sponsor, team members, and key decision-makers

  • Confirm evaluation scope, budget and delivery date for the final report.
  • Define the main roles and responsibilities for the evaluation project, including the manager who is overseeing the project and who will be taking the recommendations to senior management.
  • Identify and invite members to an Evaluation Advisory Committee.
  • Define the timelines, resources (including staff effort for interviews and data collection) and the decision process for approval of final report.

2. Define evaluation objectives and questions

  • Facilitate a meeting with the advisory committee to discuss and revise the evaluation objectives and questions.
  • Ensure evaluation questions are relevant to staff, meaningful to Black community members, and credible to funders.
  • Confirm or revise the specific activities that are delivered by the program and that are essential to the program. Include direct services to Black community members as well as other essential elements such as collaboration with other organizations, staff training and financial management.
  • Confirm or revise the key interest groups that should be engaged in data collection and/or discussion of the results.
  • Finalize the evaluation framework, confirming that the indicators are measurable and meaningful.

3. Develop and test data collection tools

  • Review and possibly adapt current client record system if one exists to assess whether it can generate reports for Black community member goals, milestones and activities. For example, every Black community member should be asked what they hope to get from the activities and supports, and if feasible asked later if they achieved their goals.
  • Draft all data collection tools (surveys, interview guides, tracking sheets). If possible, adapt and customize them from existing validated data collection tools to save time and improve validity.
  • Pilot test the tools with a small group of Black community members and staff to ensure clarity and cultural appropriateness.
  • Refine tools based on pilot feedback.

4. Collect and manage data

  • Train staff or designated data collectors on protocols to ensure data quality and consistency.
  • Deploy surveys, conduct interviews, and analyze client records as per the evaluation framework.
  • Ensure ethical protocols, including informed consent and data confidentiality, are strictly followed.
  • Frequent interim reports will assess whether the data appears to be accurate and whether it captures feedback from defined interest groups and demographic groups.
  • If necessary, additional demographic segments may be targeted with additional interviews or financial incentives.

5. Analyze data and interpret findings

  • Clean and analyze quantitative and qualitative data.
  • Deliver reports frequently to appropriate staff and interest groups with a focus on recommended actions. Emerging findings may be tested with follow-up interviews or more detailed reports.
  • Notes from staff meetings will be reviewed for evidence of actions that have been taken or recommendations that have been made as a result of the reports.
  • Hold a "sense-making" session with the advisory committee and staff to interpret the initial findings, discuss implications, and co-develop recommendations. This participatory step is crucial for ensuring the results are understood and owned by the team.

6. Communicate findings and facilitate use

  • Draft a comprehensive evaluation report with a clear executive summary.
  • Develop tailored communication materials for different groups (e.g., one-page summary for the board, presentation for staff, stories for a newsletter).
  • Facilitate a final meeting to discuss the report and create an action plan for implementing the recommendations.

Roles and responsibilities

It is necessary to involve senior managers and decision-makers in the evaluation process so that the resulting information will be relevant and credible to them. They should be engaged at three stages:

  • Defining objectives, roles and key interest groups at the beginning of the evaluation.
  • Engaging with data as soon as it starts to be collected and then occasionally throughout the project.
  • Engaging with the final recommendations and action plans near the end of the evaluation.

In addition, representatives of the organization need to be involved more deeply in:

  • Defining the program services and key processes.
  • Defining project timelines and tracking quality.
  • Configuring the client record system and refining the data collection tools.

And of course members of key interest groups will be asked for input as part of the data collection activities.

There are four essential roles for a successful evaluation project:

Sponsor (generally a senior manager who will be responsible for implementing recommendations)

  • Provide guidance regarding overall objectives and constraints of project
  • Liaise with the organization's senior management and manage organizational expectations and scope issues as appropriate
  • Communicate with internal and external interest groups regarding project progress
  • Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Liaison
  • Approve significant changes to the project scope, timeline, budget, or quality if required
  • Review and approve project documents and other deliverables

Liaison (generally a manager at the organization reporting to the Project Sponsor)

  • Act as the primary contact person with the evaluator
  • Liaise with the Project Sponsor and take on their responsibilities as delegated
  • Act as a project manager from the organization's side, e.g., scheduling meetings with program staff, negotiating with I.T. staff

Project Owner (generally a senior external evaluator)

  • Provide project leadership for the evaluation as a whole
  • Define project methodologies and advise on strategic issues
  • Author, review and approve project documents as assigned
  • Manage and resolve team-level risks, issues, and changes
  • Remove roadblocks to project success and respond to project risks and problems as they are identified by the Project Manager or Project Sponsor
  • Review and provide detailed feedback regarding all project documents and deliverables

Project Manager (generally an external evaluator reporting to the Project Owner)

  • Act as liaison to the organization for operational issues
  • Monitor project scope, quality, schedule, resources, costs and risks
  • Coordinate implementation of project work
  • Ensure project plan, schedule, and budget are up-to-date; detect and manage discrepancies
  • Report risks, delays and problems to Project Owner and Project Sponsor as they arise
  • Author, review and approve project documents as assigned to ensure the quality standards are met
  • Arrange and follow-up on team meetings
  • Manage and contribute to data collection, analysis and report writing
  • Provide leadership and manage work as appropriate

Program staff (one or more representatives of the people who will actually implement service improvements and experience changes in their workplace)

Other common roles include I.T. specialists, program Black community members and representatives from key constituencies.
